4. Project Architecture: Think in Three Separate Water Systems
Luke should resist the temptation to put every water idea into one tank. Water quality, ownership/regulation, treatment needs, and failure modes vary by source. Separate systems can still support each other through protected and controlled connections.
Layer	Sources/components	Best uses	Design note
System A: Roof rainwater	House roof, gutters, downspouts, first flush, screened conveyance, clean tank	House backup, potable after treatment, garden, emergency reserve, possibly livestock	Relatively clean source; can be protected and treated.
System B: Existing well	Deep well, pump, pressure tank, controls, wellhead, aquifer	Existing domestic/ranch supply, backup fill, emergency support	May be the most reliable source but should be protected from overuse and contamination.
System C: Whole-ranch hydrology	Road runoff, draws, ponds, stock tanks, grass cover, terraces, swales, check dams, crossings	Livestock, wildlife, erosion control, recharge, drought resilience, fire water	Most important for 500 acres, but lower water quality and different legal/engineering issues.

Recommended conceptual flow for roof rainwater
Roof -> gutters -> leaf screens/downspout filters -> first-flush diversion -> conveyance pipe or wet-line system -> tank inlet screen/filter -> calm inlet -> storage tank -> floating intake -> pump/pressure controls -> treatment if potable -> isolated plumbing or dedicated non-potable distribution -> overflow to stable drainage area.
Recommended conceptual flow for well integration
Existing well -> well pump -> pressure tank/controls -> existing domestic plumbing. If rainwater is connected to the house, the two sources should be separated by code-compliant backflow protection and a clear source-selection strategy. Do not connect rainwater, well water, and non-potable lines informally.
Recommended conceptual flow for ranch-scale runoff
Ridges and roads -> slowed runoff -> spread water across pasture where possible -> stabilized draws/check structures -> properly designed ponds/stock tanks where appropriate -> protected spillways -> overflow paths that do not erode roads, tank pads, or neighboring property. This should be planned separately from clean roof-water storage.
5. Rainfall, Catchment, and Preliminary Water-Yield Math
The water math matters because tank size should be matched to both supply and demand. Storage does not create water; catchment area creates water. The tank only stores what the catchment can deliver.
Core formula
A common rainwater harvesting formula is:
Gallons collected = rainfall inches x catchment area in square feet x 0.623 x runoff coefficient

Texas A&M AgriLife materials and calculators use this basic relationship and provide tools to evaluate whether storage and supplemental water are adequate over time. See sources [S2] and [S3].
Evant-area annual rainfall assumption
For early planning, use approximately 30.7 inches/year as an Evant-area average annual precipitation figure, but Luke should replace this with the best parcel-specific climate normal and drought-model assumptions before final sizing. Source [S4] reports 30.71 inches average annual precipitation for Evant. Average rainfall is not a design guarantee; storage should be checked against dry months and drought years.
Preliminary roof yield examples
Scenario	Calculation	Approximate result
1 inch rain, 3,000 sq. ft. roof, no losses	1 x 3,000 x 0.623	1,869 gallons
1 inch rain, 3,000 sq. ft., 75% efficiency	1,869 x 0.75	1,402 gallons
1 inch rain, 3,000 sq. ft., 85% efficiency	1,869 x 0.85	1,589 gallons
1 inch rain, 3,000 sq. ft., 90% efficiency	1,869 x 0.90	1,682 gallons
30.71 inches/year, 3,000 sq. ft., 75% efficiency	30.71 x 3,000 x 0.623 x 0.75	43,050 gallons/year approx.
30.71 inches/year, 3,000 sq. ft., 85% efficiency	30.71 x 3,000 x 0.623 x 0.85	48,790 gallons/year approx.
30.71 inches/year, 3,000 sq. ft., 90% efficiency	30.71 x 3,000 x 0.623 x 0.90	51,660 gallons/year approx.

Why average annual yield can mislead
- Rainfall is episodic. One intense storm can overflow an undersized tank while a long dry period can empty an oversized one.
- Tank sizing should be modeled month-by-month or day-by-day using rainfall records, demand, overflow, and starting tank level.
- A system that produces 49,000 gallons/year on average does not necessarily provide 134 gallons every day.
- The more critical the use, the more conservative the drought assumption should be.
- Irrigation demand usually occurs during the same hot/dry season when rainwater inflow is weakest.

8. Tank Pad, Foundation, and Site Work
The foundation is not a side issue. It is part of the tank. A full tank can weigh as much as a small building, and the load must remain level, supported, drained, and protected from erosion.
Why "no concrete base" can still require engineering
A concrete slab is not always required, but a designed base is always required. A large tank base may be compacted select fill, crushed limestone, sand bedding, geotextile, a ring wall, concrete footing, or an engineered slab depending on tank type, soil, slope, and manufacturer requirements.
Issue	Why it matters	What Luke should do
Levelness	Large tanks tolerate very little uneven settlement.	Survey pad elevations before delivery; follow manufacturer tolerance.
Bearing capacity	Soils must support concentrated full-tank load.	Evaluate clay, shallow rock, fill, slope, and drainage.
Drainage	Water around pad can erode support and undermine tank.	Crown pad, daylight drains, route overflow, armor outlet.
Access	Delivery truck, crane, skid steer, gravel truck, and future maintenance need access.	Design road/turnaround before delivery day.
Overflow protection	A full tank overflowing during a storm can dump high flow next to the pad.	Pipe overflow to stable area with riprap or level spreader.
Erosion control	Tank site may concentrate runoff.	Use swales, berms, rock, vegetation, and spillway thinking.
Animal/vehicle protection	Livestock rubbing or vehicles backing into valves can damage system.	Use fencing, bollards, valve boxes, and protected manifolds.

9. Gutters, Downspouts, and 100-Yard Conveyance Line
The system begins at the roof. If gutters, downspouts, and conveyance cannot handle intense storms, the tank will not receive the water you think it will. Texas rainfall can arrive fast, so peak-flow capacity matters.
Questions for the existing gutter system
[ ] Are gutters continuous and sloped correctly?
[ ] Are gutters undersized for roof area and local storm intensity?
[ ] Are there leaf guards? Do they shed heavy rainfall or block collection?
[ ] Can downspouts be enlarged or added?
[ ] Are downspouts currently connected to drain pipes or splash blocks?
[ ] Can every target downspout be routed to the same trunk line?
[ ] Will roof debris, pollen, acorns, asphalt granules, or bird droppings create maintenance problems?
[ ] Are there trees over the roof that increase debris load?

These are rough planning examples, not final pipe design. Final conveyance sizing should consider roof layout, downspout count, rainfall intensity, pipe slope, friction, cleanouts, trapped water, sediment, and whether the pipe operates open-channel or pressurized/wet-line.
Gravity line versus wet-line
Conveyance type	Description	Advantages	Risks
Gravity-drained line	Pipe continuously slopes from roof collection to tank and drains after storm.	Simpler, less stagnant water, fewer freeze/mosquito issues.	Requires tank inlet lower than collection points and no dips.
Wet-line system	Pipe has low points and remains full between storms; water rises into tank when enough head exists.	Can collect from downspouts where continuous slope is hard.	Needs cleanouts, drains, mosquito control, freeze consideration, sediment management, and careful sealing.
Pumped conveyance to tank	Roof water collected in sump/basin and pumped to tank.	Can overcome elevation constraints.	Adds power dependence, pump maintenance, controls, and failure risk during storms.

Tank water quality rules of thumb
- Keep sunlight, insects, animals, and organic debris out of the tank.
- Keep sediment accumulation low and provide a way to inspect/clean the tank.
- Do not place the tank where floodwater or dirty surface runoff can enter it.
- Never allow livestock to access or rub on potable/clean storage components.
- Do not use questionable roof coatings or materials for potable water without confirming suitability.
- For potable use, rely on actual lab testing and a defined treatment train, not appearance or taste.
11. Pumping Back Uphill: Pressure, Flow, and Controls
The pump design depends on total dynamic head, desired flow, desired pressure, pipe diameter, friction loss, elevation difference, and duty cycle. Do not size the pump by horsepower alone.
Core pump math
Static elevation head: every 2.31 feet of vertical lift requires about 1 psi just to lift the water. This is before friction loss and before useful pressure at the destination.
Required pump pressure approx. = elevation lift psi + friction loss psi + desired pressure at use point

Pump design data Luke must collect
[ ] Vertical rise from normal tank water level to highest point of use.
[ ] Horizontal pipe distance from tank to house/garden/trough/fire point.
[ ] Pipe material and intended pipe diameter.
[ ] Desired flow rate in gallons per minute for each use.
[ ] Desired pressure at fixture or manifold.
[ ] Whether the system will run one use at a time or multiple uses simultaneously.
[ ] Electrical power available: voltage, phase, amperage, distance to service.
[ ] Need for generator, battery, solar, or manual bypass during outages.
[ ] Control preference: manual switch, pressure switch/pressure tank, VFD constant pressure, float switches, app monitoring.
[ ] Low-level cutoff strategy to protect pump and preserve reserve water.

12. Potable Versus Non-Potable Design
This is one of the most important project forks. A non-potable ranch water system can be relatively simple. A potable or household-integrated system requires disciplined treatment, testing, plumbing isolation, backflow protection, maintenance, and documentation.
Decision tree
Question	If yes	Design consequence
Will people drink it, cook with it, brush teeth with it, or wash dishes with it?	Yes	Design as potable. Use treatment, testing, and plumbing/backflow review.
Will it supply showers, sinks, laundry, or toilets?	Yes	Decide whether potable or non-potable indoor use is allowed and permitted; plumbing separation matters.
Will it be used only for garden, hose bibs, troughs, trees, dust control, or equipment washing?	Yes	Non-potable may be appropriate; label lines and prevent cross-connection.
Will it connect to the existing well-fed house plumbing?	Yes	Require professional plumbing/backflow design.
Will it ever be used by guests or renters?	Yes	Be conservative; label and treat appropriately; document operation.

Potable treatment train concept
A typical potable rainwater treatment concept may include: protected roof catchment -> debris screening -> first flush -> clean storage -> sediment filtration -> carbon if needed -> fine filtration -> disinfection with UV and/or chlorination -> post-treatment monitoring and water testing. The exact treatment train depends on water quality, use, local code, and maintenance capacity. TCEQ notes that anyone using rainwater for drinking, cooking, or dishwashing should consider rainfall, collection area, storage, and treatment needed to make it safe. See source [S1].
Non-potable design discipline
[ ] Label non-potable hose bibs and lines.
[ ] Avoid cross-connections with well or household potable plumbing.
[ ] Use backflow prevention where there is any connection risk.
[ ] Filter enough to protect pumps, valves, drip irrigation, and emitters.
[ ] Do not let non-potable systems look like drinking-water outlets for guests or children.
[ ] Keep system drawings and valve labels clear.
Water testing plan

Stored rainwater before potable use	Before first use and on a recurring schedule.	Bacteria, turbidity, pH, residual disinfectant if chlorinated, and any treatment-specific checks.

Texas Well Owner Network resources emphasize that private well owners are responsible for water quality and provide education/testing resources; recent TWON events screen for contaminants such as total coliform bacteria, E. coli, nitrate-nitrogen, arsenic, and salinity. See sources [S12] and [S13].

14. Whole-Ranch Water Management for 500 Acres
The roof system is useful, but the 500-acre ranch itself is the bigger water opportunity. Whole-ranch water planning should aim to slow runoff, reduce erosion, improve infiltration, support livestock/wildlife, protect roads, and store water in the right places. This is a separate but complementary project.
Do not mix clean roof water with dirty surface runoff
Surface runoff from roads, pastures, livestock areas, and draws can carry sediment, manure, pathogens, hydrocarbons, pesticides, and organic debris. 20. Recommended Construction Sequence
The sequence matters. If you trench first and then change the tank site, money is wasted. If you install the tank before the overflow and pad are right, the system is vulnerable. If you connect plumbing before water quality is tested, you create risk.
Step	Action
1	Finalize project charter and intended water uses.
2	Collect data: roof, elevations, well records, water tests, maps, photos.
3	Choose potable/non-potable architecture.
4	Model tank sizes and demand scenarios.
5	Select tank site after scoring gravity, pump head, access, foundation, overflow, and aesthetics.
6	Obtain tank manufacturer foundation requirements.
7	Confirm regulatory/plumbing/electrical/well questions.
8	Bid tank, pad, pipe, pump, treatment, and electrical scopes.
9	Stake tank site, pipe route, electrical route, overflow route.
10	Call utility locate and verify septic/well lines.
11	Build pad/foundation and overflow drainage first.
12	Install conveyance trench/pipe, downspout collection, cleanouts, first flush, and inlet protection.
13	Install tank and fittings.
14	Install pump, controls, electrical, filtration/treatment, and plumbing/backflow.
15	Flush, pressure test, leak test, verify overflow, and test pump flow/pressure.
16	If potable, disinfect/treat and lab test before use.
17	Label valves/lines, create as-built diagram, and train owner/operator.
18	Set maintenance calendar and spare parts list.

21. Operations and Maintenance Plan
A rainwater system is not set-and-forget. Maintenance should be simple enough that the ranch actually does it. Design choices should reduce maintenance burden, but not pretend it disappears.

Operating rules
- Set a minimum reserve level that routine irrigation cannot use.
- Log tank level monthly for the first year.
- Log pump run times/failures and unusual noises.
- After a long dry spell, expect first rain to be dirtier and check first-flush function.
- Do not bypass filters/disinfection for potable use.
- Do not let contractors connect temporary lines that defeat backflow protection.
- Keep a printed as-built diagram near the pressure/pump controls.
22. Risk Register: What Can Go Wrong
This project has several common failure modes. The point of a risk register is not to be pessimistic; it is to spend money once instead of twice.
Risk	Consequence	Mitigation
Oversized tank, undersized roof	Tank often low; money tied up in capacity that rarely fills.	Model catchment and demand before tank purchase.
Undersized gutters/downspouts	Water spills off roof instead of reaching tank.	Evaluate peak storm flow; add/enlarge downspouts.
Bad tank foundation	Settlement, tank damage, leaks, warranty denial.	Follow manufacturer base requirements; verify soils/drainage.
Poor overflow routing	Pad erosion, flooding, road damage.	Design stable overflow path before tank install.
Wrong pump	Low pressure, pump cycling, burned pump, high energy use.	Size for total dynamic head and actual demand.
Cross-connection	Contamination of potable/well water.	Use plumber and backflow/source isolation.
Untreated potable use	Health risk.	Design treatment and test water.
Dirty runoff in clean tank	Sediment/pathogens/maintenance nightmare.	Keep roof water separate from surface runoff.
Wet-line stagnation	Odor, mosquitoes, sediment, freeze damage.	Design cleanouts, drains, screens, and maintenance access.
Electrical shortcuts	Shock/fire/equipment damage.	Use licensed electrician and code-compliant installation.
Freeze damage	Broken valves/pipes/pump components.	Protect exposed components and plan winterization.
No maintenance owner	System gradually fails.	Assign maintenance tasks and schedule.
Regulatory surprise	Delay, fines, redesign.	Call GCD/county/TCEQ/SWCD early.
Pond/dam failure	Property damage, downstream impacts.	Use NRCS/civil engineer for land-scale impoundments.

Red flags requiring professional review
[ ] Any connection between rainwater and household potable plumbing.
[ ] Any connection between rainwater system and well system.
[ ] Any large tank on slope, fill, expansive clay, or questionable soil.
[ ] Any dam, pond, diversion, or work in/near a defined channel or creek.
[ ] Any high-flow fire water outlet or claim of fire protection.
[ ] Any electrical work beyond plug-in temporary equipment.
[ ] Any system that will be used by guests, renters, workers, or public users.
[ ] Any roof material or coating not clearly suitable for intended water use.

29. Bottom-Line Guidance
The project should be approached with ambition and restraint at the same time. The ambition is right: a 500-acre Texas ranch should have a serious water plan. The restraint is also necessary: one 3,000 sq. ft. roof is not enough to solve every ranch water need. It can be a very good clean-water source, especially for backup, garden, and resilience, but it should be one layer in a broader plan.
Luke’s job is to convert the vision into measured facts, a water balance model, a site plan, and a staged budget. The right first win is not a giant tank. The right first win is a clear design that Ben can trust before spending serious money.
